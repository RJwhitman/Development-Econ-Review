Topic 1 · Introduction to Development Economics
What is Development? · Global Inequality Facts · Themes
Definition

What is Development Economics?

Development economics seeks to understand the diverse economic and social conditions that determine the wellbeing of individuals around the globe — and what can be done to improve them. Key themes:

  • Geographic: Applying modern economic techniques to low- and middle-income countries.
  • Non-Western: Understanding institutions, family structures, social norms and preferences that don't exist in HICs.
  • Macro/Growth: Differences in macro performance between countries and over time.
  • Poverty: Why are some people poor? Why is there inequality? Subject-based, not geography-based — includes the poor in HICs too.
  • Policy Orientation: Research is motivated by a desire to improve outcomes — more prominent here than in any other field.
Facts

Global Income Inequality — Key Facts

  • The ratio of per capita income in rich vs poor countries was 1:3 at the start of industrialisation; it is now roughly 1:100.
  • Top per capita GNI (PPP, 2021): Singapore $102,450, Norway $82,840, US $70,480.
  • Bottom: Burundi $780, Central African Republic $980, DRC $1,110.
  • Poverty is geographically concentrated — heavily in the tropical belt, especially Sub-Saharan Africa. Latin America is relatively more developed; Asia and North Africa fall in between.
  • Life expectancy gap: Top countries (Hong Kong, Japan) ~85 years; bottom (Chad, Nigeria) ~53 years.
Exam Focus

Why Western Countries Are the Unusual Ones

The original framing — "Why are some countries poor?" — is now considered paternalistic. The modern reframing asks: "What is the source of the diverse set of economic and social conditions that determine the wellbeing of individuals around the globe?" This treats Western development as one outcome among many, not as the universal norm to which all countries must aspire.

Topic 2 · Defining Development
Traditional vs Modern Views · Happiness · MDGs · Sen's Capabilities
Core Concept

Traditional vs Modern Views of Development

  • Traditional view: Almost exclusively focused on per capita income (GNP/GDP). Set the right macro conditions (capital stock, monetary policy, fiscal policy, trade) and all will benefit. Emerged in 1950s-60s.
  • Modern view: Per capita income is still extremely important but too narrow. Also focus on unemployment, inequality, number of poor households, and human capital. Emerged after the 1970s-80s, when "fixing" policies raised GDP but failed to reduce poverty at the individual level.
  • Modern approaches are multidimensional — there is no single objective function to maximise.
Model

Happiness Index (Layard)

  • Unidimensional measure of subjective well-being: "Taking all things together, how happy are you?" (0-10 scale).
  • Research shows it correlates well with macro statistics, health, etc.
  • Key findings: Below ~$15k GNI/capita, wealth strongly predicts happiness. Above $15k, the slope flattens — money does less. The seven determinants of happiness (at individual level): family relationships, financial situation, work, community/friends, health, personal freedom, personal values.
  • Bhutan actually adopted Gross National Happiness (GNH) as an official goal.
Concept

Millennium & Sustainable Development Goals

  • MDGs (2000): Adopted by 189 UN members. Eight goals (eradicate poverty, universal primary education, gender equality, child mortality, maternal health, HIV/AIDS, environment, global partnership). Each with measurable criteria and a 2015 deadline — a dramatic departure from prior frameworks.
  • Criticisms: Not ambitious enough, or unachievable. No post-2015 vision. Lacked prioritisation.
  • SDGs (2016): 17 goals, target 2030. More complex, revised targets, but harder to communicate.
Core Concept

Sen's Capability Approach

Developed by Nobel laureate Amartya Sen. Moves beyond income and resources to focus on what people can actually do and be.

  • Access: Ability to access resources (e.g., having a bicycle).
  • Functioning: Ability to make use of resources (being able to ride it). Depends on a combination of resources, social environment, and political environment.
  • Capability: Ability to choose over multiple functionings — true freedom.
  • Development = expanding capabilities: Expanding access to resources (income), services (education, healthcare), and political freedoms. "Development as Freedom."
Access → Functioning → Capability
Resources → Using them → Choosing among uses

Critiques: Agency competence (do people always choose in their own interest?), community constraints (Amish, Hasidic communities restrict individual choices but may bind communities), and natural vs constructed preferences.

Topic 3 · Comparing Countries
GDP/GNI · PPP · HDI · Country Classification
Concept

Three Goals of Development Measurement

  • Describe current conditions: Accurate and representative of what is actually happening now.
  • Intertemporal comparison: Ensure the same things are measured at different points in time — requires deflating to a base year to remove inflation.
  • Cross-country comparison: Ensure the same things are measured in different countries — requires currency conversion and PPP adjustment.

These are nested: you must accurately describe current conditions before you can make valid intertemporal comparisons, which are in turn necessary for valid cross-country comparisons.

Concept

GDP vs GNI, and PPP Adjustment

  • GDP (Gross Domestic Product): Total value added within a country's borders, regardless of who owns the factors.
  • GNI (Gross National Income): Total income earned by a country's residents, regardless of where earned. GNI = GDP + net factor income from abroad.
  • PPP adjustment: Converts currencies to a common purchasing power. A basket of identical goods is priced in each country; currencies are adjusted so the basket costs the same everywhere. Needed because exchange rates don't reflect actual purchasing power (e.g., a haircut is far cheaper in Vietnam than the US).
  • 4-step standardisation: (1) Measure total income in local currency. (2) Deflate to base year (remove inflation). (3) Convert to standard currency (USD). (4) Apply PPP adjustment.
Core Formula

Human Development Index (HDI)

UNDP calculates annually. Combines three dimensions — health, education, and standard of living.

Each sub-index: $$I = \frac{\text{actual} - \text{minimum}}{\text{maximum} - \text{minimum}}$$ New HDI (2010-present): $$\text{HDI} = \left(I_{\text{Life}} \times I_{\text{Education}} \times I_{\text{Income}}\right)^{1/3}$$ Old HDI (1990-2009): $$\text{HDI} = \frac{1}{3}I_{\text{Life}} + \frac{1}{3}I_{\text{Education}} + \frac{1}{3}I_{\text{Income}}$$
  • Health: Life expectancy at birth.
  • Education: Geometric mean of average years of schooling and expected years of schooling.
  • Standard of living: Per capita GNI (log-transformed).
  • Criticisms: Sub-indexes are highly correlated with GNI, limiting multidimensionality. Easy to criticise for omissions (environment, freedom). Substitution between dimensions is arbitrary.
  • Key difference old→new: Geometric mean penalises countries with extreme imbalances — you can't compensate a terrible health score with a great income score.
Exam Focus

Country Classification Systems

  • World Bank (GNI per capita): LIC ≤ $1,025 | LMC $1,026-$4,035 | UMC $4,036-$12,475 | HIC ≥ $12,476.
  • UNDP (HDI): Low <0.550 | Medium 0.550-0.699 | High 0.700-0.799 | Very High ≥0.800.
  • UN Least Developed Countries (LDCs): Must meet three criteria — low income, low human capital, high economic vulnerability. Updated every three years by the Committee for Development Policy.

Proof

Why the Geometric Mean Penalises Imbalance (AM-GM Inequality)

Claim
For any non-negative $a, b, c$: $\left(\frac{a+b+c}{3}\right) \geq (abc)^{1/3}$, with equality iff $a=b=c$.
Step 1
Start with 2-variable AM-GM: $(a-b)^2 \geq 0 \Rightarrow a^2+b^2 \geq 2ab \Rightarrow (a+b)^2 \geq 4ab \Rightarrow \frac{a+b}{2} \geq \sqrt{ab}$.
Step 2
Extend to 3 variables using the substitution trick: Let $m = (abc)^{1/3}$. Suppose $a + b + c = 3m$. One can show via repeated application that $a = b = c = m$ is the only point where the arithmetic mean equals the geometric mean. For all other distributions, AM > GM.
Application
A country with $I_{\text{Life}}=0.9$, $I_{\text{Edu}}=0.9$, $I_{\text{Inc}}=0.1$:
Old HDI = $(0.9+0.9+0.1)/3 = 0.633$
New HDI = $(0.9 \times 0.9 \times 0.1)^{1/3} = (0.081)^{1/3} \approx 0.433$
The geometric mean correctly penalises the catastrophically low income score. The old arithmetic mean masks the imbalance.
∎ Q.E.D.
Topic 4 · Characteristics of Developing Countries
10 Shared Characteristics · Convergence Types
Concept

Ten Shared Characteristics of Developing Countries

  1. Low levels of living and productivity — low per capita income, low output per worker.
  2. Low levels of human capital — high child mortality, low schooling, poor health. Child mortality correlates strongly with maternal education.
  3. High levels of inequality and absolute poverty — more people in extreme poverty; fewer in middle class.
  4. High population growth — high crude birth rates relative to HICs.
  5. Social fractionalization — linguistic, ethnic, and religious divisions that impede collective action (especially in Africa, Middle East).
  6. Larger rural populations and fast urbanization — still majority rural but urbanising rapidly, often faster than job creation.
  7. Lower levels of industrialization — primary sectors (agriculture, extraction) dominate; manufacturing share is small.
  8. Adverse geography — landlocked, tropical disease burden (malaria), N-S continental orientation limiting agricultural diffusion, climate change exposure (Africa, South Asia).
  9. Weak institutions and underdeveloped markets — poor property rights, contract enforcement, limited access to opportunities, elite capture.
  10. Colonial legacy and external dependence — exploitative regimes (Sub-Saharan Africa) left worst institutional quality; intermediate regimes (India, South America) mixed; settler regimes (US, Canada, Australia) did best. "Reversal of fortune": wealthier pre-colonial areas were easier to exploit → post-colonial institutional damage is worse.
Concept

Types of Convergence

  • Absolute convergence: Per capita income levels are becoming more similar across countries. Requires poorer countries to grow faster AND be close enough to wealthy countries to catch up. The most important type — but hardest to achieve. Evidence is mixed at best.
  • Relative convergence: Poorer countries grow faster than wealthier ones (a precondition for absolute convergence). Requires negative correlation of growth rate with initial income. Holds within OECD countries; does NOT hold for all developing countries.
  • Population-weighted relative convergence: Relative convergence weighted by population size — prioritises China and India. Some evidence since 1976, driven largely by China's rapid growth.
  • Conditional convergence: Convergence conditional on other characteristics (urbanization, institutions, resources). Already seen in Solow model.
  • World-as-one-country convergence: Estimates income for every person on earth and asks if the global distribution is converging. Very hard to measure.
  • Sectoral convergence: Convergence in specific sectors. Dani Rodrik finds strong evidence of manufacturing sector convergence — explains manufacturing-led growth in China, India, South America.
Exam Focus

"Developing" Does Not Mean "Behind"

Today's LICs are not simply where today's HICs were 200 years ago. Key differences: colonial legacy damaged institutions; today's LICs operate in a world with existing technologies they can adopt (leap-frogging); comparative income levels are much more extreme; international migration, trade, and scientific knowledge flow in ways impossible during early industrialisation. Britain doubled worker productivity in 60 years; South Korea did it in 12, China in 9.

Topic 5 · Classic Growth Models
Production Function · Rostow · Harrod-Domar
Core Formula

Production Function & Components of Growth

$$Y = f(K, L)$$ Three determinants of growth: Capital accumulation ($K$), Population growth ($L$), Technological progress ($f(\cdot)$)
  • Capital accumulation: All new investment in physical capital, infrastructure, and human capital. Funded by diverting income into savings → investment → future output.
  • Population growth: Increases labour supply, but at the macro level large population increases can reverse the per-worker gains.
  • Technological progress: Now considered the most important long-run growth factor. Three types:
    • Neutral: Higher output with the same input combination (e.g., division of labour). Doesn't change relative marginal products.
    • Factor-saving: Allows substitution — either labour-saving (use less labour, more capital: typical in HICs where labour is expensive) or capital-saving (use less capital, more labour: more useful in LICs).
    • Factor-augmenting: Increases the marginal product of a specific factor. Labour-augmenting = better education/health. Capital-augmenting = iron replacing bronze.
Model

Rostow's Stages of Growth

  • Walter Rostow (UT professor; Special Assistant for National Security to LBJ). Popular in 1950s-60s, strongly influenced by the Marshall Plan.
  • Five stages: (1) Traditional Society → (2) Pre-conditions for Take-off → (3) Take-off → (4) Drive to Maturity → (5) Age of High Mass Consumption.
  • Mechanism: mustering savings for capital accumulation moves a country through stages.
  • All countries follow the same path (universal); there is only one path (necessary). Heavily criticised for Eurocentrism and oversimplification.
Core Formula

Harrod-Domar Growth Model

Key mechanism: capital accumulation through savings-driven investment.

Production: $Y_t = \frac{1}{c} K_t$  (capital-output ratio $c = K/Y$)
Savings: $S_t = s \cdot Y_t$
Investment: $I_t = S_t = s \cdot Y_t$
Capital accumulation: $\Delta K = I_t - \delta K_t = sY_t - \delta K_t$

$$\boxed{g = \frac{\Delta Y}{Y} = \frac{s}{c} - \delta}$$ where $s$ = savings rate, $c$ = capital-output ratio (ICOR), $\delta$ = depreciation rate
  • To grow faster: raise $s$ or reduce $c$ (use capital more efficiently).
  • Savings gap: if domestic savings are insufficient, foreign aid or FDI fills the gap: $\Delta Y/Y = (s + f)/c - \delta$ where $f$ = foreign capital inflow as share of GDP.
  • Limitations: Assumes fixed $c$ (Leontief production function — no substitution). In practice, $c$ varies and savings don't automatically translate into investment. Doesn't account for technology or labour.

Proof

Deriving $g = s/c - \delta$ in Harrod-Domar

Step 1
From the fixed capital-output ratio: $Y_t = K_t / c$, so $\Delta Y_t = \Delta K_t / c$.
Step 2
Capital accumulation: $\Delta K_t = I_t - \delta K_t = S_t - \delta K_t = sY_t - \delta K_t$.
Step 3
Divide $\Delta K_t$ by $c$ to get $\Delta Y_t$:
$$\Delta Y_t = \frac{sY_t - \delta K_t}{c} = \frac{sY_t}{c} - \frac{\delta K_t}{c} = \frac{s}{c}Y_t - \delta Y_t$$
(using $K_t = cY_t$ in the last step)
Step 4
Divide both sides by $Y_t$:
$$g = \frac{\Delta Y_t}{Y_t} = \frac{s}{c} - \delta$$
Growth rate is linear in the savings rate, inversely proportional to the capital-output ratio (ICOR), and reduced by depreciation.
∎ Q.E.D.
Topic 6 · Lewis Two-Sector Model
Agricultural vs Manufacturing · Migration Dynamics · Turning Point · Limitations
Background

Structural Change Models

Lewis (1954, Nobel 1979) developed the first formal structural-change model. These models organise the economy into sectors and study how the composition of activity changes with development.

  • Two sectors: Rural/Agricultural and Urban/Manufacturing.
  • Economy is closed (no trade, no foreign investment).
  • Labour moves freely between sectors.
  • Further developed by Fei and Ranis.
Core Model

Agricultural Sector Assumptions

  • Large surplus of labour — so large that MPL = 0 (adding or removing a worker doesn't change output).
  • No competitive labour market. Wages set at the average product of labour: $w_A = Y_A / L_A$ (food produced and shared equally).
  • Capital stock is fixed; no investment incentive (MPL = 0 means no return to hiring more workers).
  • Migration to urban areas does not affect the agricultural wage (surplus labour cushion).
$$w_A = \frac{Y_A}{L_A} = APL_A \qquad \text{(subsistence wage)}$$
Core Model

Manufacturing Sector Assumptions

  • Competitive labour market: wages equal MPL. No unemployment (anyone can find work at the prevailing wage).
  • Capital is labour-augmenting: increasing capital raises MPL and thus the competitive wage.
  • All profits are reinvested into capital — firms grow by expanding capital and hiring more workers.
$$w_M = MPL_M(K) \qquad \text{(competitive wage)}$$ As $K \uparrow$: $MPL_M \uparrow \Rightarrow w_M \uparrow$
Core Model

Model Dynamics — The Two-Stage Cycle

The model proceeds in periods, each with two stages:

  1. Migration Stage: $w_M > w_A$ → labour moves from agriculture to manufacturing, pushing $w_M$ down until $w_M = w_A + \text{urban premium}$.
  2. Production/Investment Stage: Additional labour raises manufacturing output → profits → capital investment → higher MPL → higher $w_M$ above $w_A$ → triggers next migration stage.

This self-reinforcing cycle continues as long as the agricultural surplus labour cushion holds. Growth is endogenous — savings and investment drive it without exogenous technology.

Key Concept

Lewis Turning Point & Self-Sustaining Growth

  • Lewis Turning Point: Eventually the agricultural surplus labour is exhausted. Further migration now raises agricultural wages (MPL > 0 as labour becomes scarce). The unlimited labour supply assumption breaks down.
  • After the turning point: further labour migration requires higher wages → manufacturing profits fall → slower capital accumulation. Labour market becomes fully competitive across sectors.
  • Declining MPK: Capital deepening raises output but by ever-smaller amounts — $f''(K) < 0$ — eventually slowing growth. This is the other brake on self-sustaining growth.
  • China's economy is often cited as a modern example of the Lewis process.
Limitations

Limitations of the Lewis Model

  • Agricultural sector: Surplus labour is often cyclical (seasonal), not permanent. Rural wages do tend to be set by MPL, not APL. MPK in agriculture is positive — better seeds, equipment, and techniques raise output.
  • Manufacturing sector: Capital need not be labour-augmenting. If firms adopt labour-saving technology, profits flow to capital owners rather than workers — worsening inequality without creating employment.
  • Urban unemployment: Lewis assumes anyone can find work in manufacturing. In reality, migrants often can't — this motivates Harris-Todaro (Topic 16).
  • Underlying insight survives: As long as (1) return to capital is higher in manufacturing, and (2) capital investments are neutral or labour-augmenting, the core dynamic of capital-driven migration holds.

Lewis Cycle — One Full Period

Initial state: $K=100$, $L_M=20$, $Y_M = K^{0.3}L_M^{0.7} = 100^{0.3} \cdot 20^{0.7}$. Agricultural wage $w_A = 2$. Savings rate $s=0.3$, depreciation $\delta=0.05$.

$Y_M \approx 3.98 \times 8.10 \approx 32.2$  (manufacturing output)

Profit = $Y_M - w_A \cdot L_M = 32.2 - 2 \times 20 = -7.8$ → not yet profitable

$Y_M \approx 4.74 \times 8.10 \approx 38.4$

Topic 7 · Neoclassical Models
Solow Growth Model · Counter-Revolution · TFP
Background

Neoclassical Counter-Revolution

A trend in economic theory in the 1980s-90s emphasizing free-market efficiency. Key beliefs: end price/wage controls, open economies to trade, remove currency controls. The Solow model is the central example of these neoclassical models.

Core Model

Solow Growth Model — Production Function

Developed by Robert Solow (MIT, published 1956). Models the relationship between investment, capital deepening, and output growth.

$$Y = K^\alpha (AL)^{1-\alpha}$$ $$y = f(k) = A k^\alpha$$ where $y = Y/L$, $k = K/L$, $\tilde{A} = A^{1-\alpha}$
Key Equation

Change in Per Capita Capital Stock

$$\Delta k = sf(k) - (\delta + n)k$$
$\Delta k > 0$ if $sf(k) > (\delta+n)k$  → capital growing
$\Delta k < 0$ if $sf(k) < (\delta+n)k$  → capital shrinking
$\Delta k = 0$ if $sf(k) = (\delta+n)k$  → equilibrium $k^*$
  • s = savings rate; δ = depreciation; n = population growth
  • Equilibrium k* is a stable equilibrium — the model pulls capital back if disturbed.
Proof 1

Deriving $\Delta k = sf(k) - (\delta + n)k$ from First Principles

Given
The capital accumulation equation in aggregate terms: new capital = investment minus depreciation.
$$K_{t+1} = K_t + sY_t - \delta K_t$$
Investment equals savings $sY_t$; depreciation erodes at rate $\delta$.
Step 1
Divide through by population $L_t$ to convert to per capita terms. Define $k_t = K_t/L_t$ and $y_t = Y_t/L_t$:
$$\frac{K_{t+1}}{L_t} = k_t + sf(k_t) - \delta k_t$$
Step 2
Population grows at rate $n$, so $L_{t+1} = (1+n)L_t$. Write $k_{t+1}$ in terms of $K_{t+1}$ and $L_{t+1}$:
$$k_{t+1} = \frac{K_{t+1}}{L_{t+1}} = \frac{K_{t+1}}{(1+n)L_t}$$
Step 3
Substitute the expression for $K_{t+1}$ from Step 1:
$$k_{t+1} = \frac{k_t + sf(k_t) - \delta k_t}{1+n} = \frac{(1-\delta)k_t + sf(k_t)}{1+n}$$
Step 4
The change in per capita capital is $\Delta k = k_{t+1} - k_t$:
$$\Delta k = \frac{(1-\delta)k_t + sf(k_t)}{1+n} - k_t = \frac{sf(k_t) - \delta k_t - nk_t - n\delta k_t}{1+n}$$
Step 5
Since $n$ and $\delta$ are both small, the cross-term $n\delta k_t \approx 0$ is negligible, and $(1+n) \approx 1$. This gives the standard approximation:
$$\boxed{\Delta k \approx sf(k) - (\delta + n)k}$$
∎ Q.E.D.
Proof 2

Closed-Form $k^*$ and Proof of Stability

Setup
Use $f(k) = Ak^\alpha$. Set $\Delta k = 0$:
$$sAk^\alpha = (\delta+n)k$$
Step 1
Divide both sides by $k$ (valid since $k > 0$):
$$sA k^{\alpha-1} = \delta + n$$
Step 2
Isolate $k^{\alpha-1}$ then raise both sides to the power $\frac{1}{\alpha-1}$. Since $\alpha < 1$, the exponent $\frac{1}{\alpha-1}$ is negative — flip the fraction:
$$k^{\alpha-1} = \frac{\delta+n}{sA} \implies \boxed{k^* = \left(\frac{sA}{\delta+n}\right)^{\!\frac{1}{1-\alpha}}}$$
Stability
Define $\phi(k) = sf(k) - (\delta+n)k$. We need $\phi'(k^*) < 0$ for stability.
$$\phi'(k) = sf'(k) - (\delta+n) = s\alpha A k^{\alpha-1} - (\delta+n)$$
At $k^*$, we know $sAk^{*\alpha-1} = \delta+n$, so $s\alpha A k^{*\alpha-1} = \alpha(\delta+n)$. Therefore:
$$\phi'(k^*) = \alpha(\delta+n) - (\delta+n) = (\alpha-1)(\delta+n)$$
Since $0 < \alpha < 1$, we have $\alpha - 1 < 0$ and $\delta+n > 0$, so $\phi'(k^*) < 0$. The equilibrium is locally stable.
∎ Q.E.D.
Exam Focus

Self-Sustaining Growth — The Key Conclusion

  • Increasing savings rate s shifts equilibrium from k* to k*' (higher output) — but this is a one-time level shift, not sustained growth.
  • Savings rate is bounded: s < 1, and people need to consume.
  • Conclusion: Only technological change (not capital accumulation) can produce continuous long-run growth. Very different from Harrod-Domar and Lewis models.
Concept

Solow Residual (Total Factor Productivity)

  • Growth is decomposed into: changes in capital, changes in labor, and technological change.
  • The Solow Residual = portion of growth NOT explained by capital or labor = attributed to technology.
  • Also called Total Factor Productivity (TFP) — it increases all marginal products equally.
  • Accounts for roughly 50% of economic growth.
Limitations

Limitations of the Solow Model

  • Tells us nothing about the determinants of technological change — yet tech explains ~half of growth.
  • Countries with similar technologies often have different residuals.
  • Empirical estimates show economies take decades to reach equilibrium after savings rate changes — raises doubt about whether equilibrium is ever truly reached.

Topic 8 · Endogenous Growth Models
Romer Model · Increasing Returns · Spillovers
Concept

Endogenous vs Exogenous Variables

  • Exogenous: Values assumed/given (e.g., savings rate in Harrod-Domar).
  • Endogenous: Values functionally derived from model dynamics (e.g., growth rate in Harrod-Domar).
  • Solow is called an exogenous growth model because the determining factor of long-run growth (technology A) is exogenous — even though growth itself is derived within the model.
Key Idea

Endogenous Growth Theory

  • Motivated by Solow's limitations — seeks to explain all factors of growth within one model.
  • Relaxes the classic assumption of constant/diminishing returns to scale.
  • Evidence for increasing returns: capital-intensive manufacturing, education externalities, technological dissemination/copying between firms.
  • Allowing increasing returns creates a feedback loop where returns to investment increase as investment increases → self-sustaining growth.
Model

Romer Model — Overview

  • Developed by Paul Romer (Nobel laureate, World Bank Chief Economist).
  • Key papers: 1986 and 1990.
  • Models technological change as "learning by doing" — capital investments by firms create knowledge that benefits all firms (spillovers = public good).
Core Formula

Romer Aggregate Production Function

FIRM LEVEL
$$Y_i = A K_i^\alpha L_i^{1-\alpha} \bar{K}^\beta \quad \text{where } \bar{K} = \tfrac{1}{N}\textstyle\sum_{i=1}^N K_i$$
AGGREGATE
$$Y = A K^{\alpha+\beta} L^{1-\alpha} \quad 0 < \alpha < 1,\; 1-\alpha > \beta > 0$$

The term K^β is the endogenous component — it models spillovers where firm-level investments generate knowledge usable by all firms.

Derivation

Long-Run Growth Equation

$$g = \frac{n(1-\alpha)}{1-\alpha-\beta}$$
Capital law of motion: $\dot{K} = sY - \delta K$
  • If β = 0 (no spillovers), then g = n — same as Solow (per capita income constant long-run).
  • If β > 0, growth exceeds population growth — self-sustaining growth is possible.
  • Requires 1−α−β > 0 for the model to be stable.
Proof 1

Proving $\dot{Y}/Y = \dot{K}/K = g$ in Steady State

Given
Capital law of motion: $\dot{K} = sY - \delta K$. Divide both sides by $K$:
$$\frac{\dot{K}}{K} = s\frac{Y}{K} - \delta$$
Step 1
In steady state, $\dot{K}/K$ is constant. For this to hold, $Y/K$ must also be constant. Call the constant ratio $a$, so $Y = aK$ for all $t$.
Step 2
Differentiate $Y = aK$ with respect to time:
$$\dot{Y} = a\dot{K}$$
Step 3
Divide both sides by $Y = aK$:
$$\frac{\dot{Y}}{Y} = \frac{a\dot{K}}{aK} = \frac{\dot{K}}{K}$$
Therefore in steady state $\dot{Y}/Y = \dot{K}/K \equiv g$. $\checkmark$
∎ Q.E.D.
Proof 2

Full Derivation of $g = \dfrac{n(1-\alpha)}{1-\alpha-\beta}$

Given
Aggregate production function: $Y = AK^{\alpha+\beta}L^{1-\alpha}$
Step 1
Compute the partial derivatives of $Y$ with respect to $K$ and $L$:
$$\frac{\partial Y}{\partial K} = A(\alpha+\beta)K^{\alpha+\beta-1}L^{1-\alpha} = \frac{(\alpha+\beta)Y}{K}$$ $$\frac{\partial Y}{\partial L} = A(1-\alpha)K^{\alpha+\beta}L^{-\alpha} = \frac{(1-\alpha)Y}{L}$$
Step 2
Use the chain rule to differentiate $Y(K(t), L(t))$ with respect to time:
$$\dot{Y} = \frac{\partial Y}{\partial K}\dot{K} + \frac{\partial Y}{\partial L}\dot{L} = \frac{(\alpha+\beta)Y}{K}\dot{K} + \frac{(1-\alpha)Y}{L}\dot{L}$$
Step 3
Divide through by $Y$ to express in growth rates:
$$\frac{\dot{Y}}{Y} = (\alpha+\beta)\frac{\dot{K}}{K} + (1-\alpha)\frac{\dot{L}}{L}$$
Step 4
Substitute steady-state values $\dot{Y}/Y = g$, $\dot{K}/K = g$ (from Proof 1), and $\dot{L}/L = n$:
$$g = (\alpha+\beta)g + (1-\alpha)n$$
Step 5
Collect $g$ terms on the left:
$$g - (\alpha+\beta)g = (1-\alpha)n$$ $$g\bigl[1 - (\alpha+\beta)\bigr] = (1-\alpha)n$$ $$g(1-\alpha-\beta) = (1-\alpha)n$$
Step 6
Divide both sides by $(1-\alpha-\beta) > 0$:
$$\boxed{g = \frac{n(1-\alpha)}{1-\alpha-\beta}}$$
∎ Q.E.D.
Exam Focus

FDI & Convergence Predictions

  • Solow: FDI flows to poorer countries (lower tech → higher returns). Countries converge if technologies & savings equalize.
  • Romer: FDI flows to wealthier countries (increasing returns → highest returns where capital stocks already high). No guarantee of convergence. Recessions can permanently set countries back.
  • Romer better fits the real-world observation that FDI flows disproportionately to HICs.

Suppose $\alpha = 0.5$, $\beta = 0.2$, $n = 0.03$. Find the long-run growth rate $g$.

Topic 9 · Equilibria & Coordination Failure
Nash Equilibrium · Kerala Fish Markets · Multiple Equilibria
Game Theory

Nash Equilibrium

A combination of strategies where no individual player has an incentive to deviate, given that all others play the equilibrium strategy.

  • More general than Dominant Strategy Equilibrium (DSE) — only requires best response to equilibrium play, not all undominated actions.
  • All DSE are Nash equilibria, but not vice versa.
  • Games can have multiple Nash equilibria (e.g., Battle of the Sexes has two).
Example

Prisoner's Dilemma vs. Battle of the Sexes

  • Prisoner's Dilemma: Has a dominant strategy equilibrium (both confess) — players always confess regardless of opponent's choice.
  • Battle of the Sexes: No DSE, but two Nash equilibria: (Opera, Opera) and (Fight, Fight).
Case Study

Kerala Fish Markets (Jensen 2007)

  • Fishermen and buyers must choose markets, but lack of communication prevents coordination on the more profitable equilibrium.
  • Fish are sometimes discarded due to poor coordination.
  • Mobile phone introduction → better coordination → prices stabilized, profits rose.
  • Illustrates: costs of bad equilibria, difficulty of coordinating toward better ones.
Core Model

Multiple Equilibria Model

  • Uses best response functions in a two-player symmetric game.
  • Nash Equilibrium condition: Y* = f(Y*) — player's best response to opponent's equilibrium action is that same action.
  • Model has three equilibria: two stable, one unstable.
  • Stable equilibria: Robust to small deviations — system returns to equilibrium. (Like k* in Solow.)
  • Unstable equilibrium: Any deviation causes shift to a different equilibrium.
Proof

Stability Condition for Nash Equilibria in the Best-Response Model

Setup
In a symmetric two-player game, the Nash Equilibrium condition is $Y^* = f(Y^*)$, where $f$ is the best-response function. A NE is stable if, after a small perturbation $\varepsilon$, the system returns to $Y^*$.
Step 1
Suppose player 2 deviates slightly: $Y_2 = Y^* + \varepsilon$. Player 1's best response is:
$$Y_1 = f(Y^* + \varepsilon) \approx f(Y^*) + f'(Y^*)\varepsilon = Y^* + f'(Y^*)\varepsilon$$
The deviation from $Y^*$ in player 1's response is $f'(Y^*)\cdot\varepsilon$.
Step 2
For the system to return to equilibrium, the response to the perturbation must shrink — meaning each round of best-response adjustment brings players closer to $Y^*$. This requires the deviation to shrink:
$$|f'(Y^*)| < 1 \implies \text{stable equilibrium}$$
Conversely, if $|f'(Y^*)| > 1$, the deviation grows and the equilibrium is unstable.
Graphically
Plot $Y_1 = f(Y_2)$ and the 45° line $Y_1 = Y_2$. Nash equilibria are intersections.
  • Where $f$ cuts through the 45° line from above (slope of $f$ < 1): stable — deviations are self-correcting.
  • Where $f$ cuts through the 45° line from below (slope of $f$ > 1): unstable — deviations amplify.
In the multiple equilibria model: the two outer intersections are stable; the middle intersection is unstable because $f$ crosses the 45° line from below there.
Intuition
Think of the unstable equilibrium as a ball balanced on a hilltop — any nudge sends it rolling toward one of the stable valleys (the two stable NE). The stable equilibria are like valleys — the ball returns after small pushes.
∎ Q.E.D.

Topic 10 · Big Push Model
Poverty Traps · Modernization · Pecuniary Externalities
Overview

Big Push — Core Ideas

  • Combines Nash Equilibrium + multiple equilibria to model economy-wide industrialization decisions.
  • Three possible outcomes: destined to modernize (HICs/W1), require coordination (Asian Tigers/W2), cannot modernize alone (LICs/W3).
  • Demonstrates poverty trap: countries can be stuck in a bad (low-wage, low-production) equilibrium.
  • Key concept: pecuniary externalities — spillovers from one firm's decisions to another's costs/revenues.
Model Assumptions

Economy Structure

  • Closed economy, N industries; output priced at 1 (numeraire).
  • Labor L equally divided: L/N per industry; wages differ by firm type.
  • Only choice: traditional vs. modern manufacturing.
Production

Traditional vs. Modern Firms

TRADITIONAL
$$Q_t(L) = L \qquad C(Q) = w_t \cdot Q = Q \quad (w_t = 1)$$
MODERN
$$L = F + cQ \quad (0 < c < 1)$$ $$Q_m(L) = \tfrac{1}{c}L - \tfrac{1}{c}F \qquad W_i(Q) = w_i(F + cQ)$$
  • Modern firms have higher marginal product (1/c > 1) but require fixed cost F.
  • Modern firms monopolize their sector (threat-of-entry pricing).
  • Due to symmetry, only Nash equilibria are: all modernize OR none modernize.
Proof 1

Deriving the Modern Firm's Production Function

Given
The total labor required to produce $Q$ units in a modern firm is:
$$L = F + cQ \quad \text{where } F > 0,\; 0 < c < 1$$
$F$ is the fixed overhead labor (to operate the machine); $cQ$ is the variable component.
Step 1
Invert $L = F + cQ$ to express output as a function of labor:
$$cQ = L - F \implies Q_m(L) = \frac{1}{c}L - \frac{1}{c}F = \frac{L-F}{c}$$
Step 2
The marginal product of labor in the modern sector is:
$$\frac{dQ_m}{dL} = \frac{1}{c}$$
Since $0 < c < 1$, we have $1/c > 1$. Modern firms produce more than one unit of output per unit of labor — strictly higher productivity than the traditional sector where $dQ_t/dL = 1$.
Step 3
For a given industry labor supply $L/N$, compare outputs:
$$Q_m\!\left(\frac{L}{N}\right) = \frac{1}{c}\frac{L}{N} - \frac{F}{c} \qquad Q_t\!\left(\frac{L}{N}\right) = \frac{L}{N}$$ $$Q_m > Q_t \iff \frac{1}{c}\frac{L}{N} - \frac{F}{c} > \frac{L}{N} \iff \frac{L}{N}\!\left(\frac{1}{c}-1\right) > \frac{F}{c}$$
This holds when $L/N$ is large enough — i.e., when labor supply per industry is sufficient to cover the fixed cost overhead.
∎ Q.E.D.
Proof 2

Only Symmetric Outcomes Can Be Nash Equilibria

Setup
There are $N$ identical firms, each choosing between Traditional ($T$) or Modern ($M$). Suppose a candidate Nash Equilibrium has $k$ firms playing $M$ and $N-k$ playing $T$, where $0 < k < N$ (a mixed outcome).
Step 1
Consider one of the $k$ modernizing firms. Its payoff from playing $M$ is $\pi_M(k)$ (depends on how many others also modernize, which determines aggregate demand and wages).
Step 2
Because all firms are identical, they face the same payoff function. If playing $M$ is optimal for one firm given the others' strategies, it must be optimal for all firms in the same position. Therefore a firm playing $T$ faces the same calculation — and should also switch to $M$.
Step 3
This means a traditional firm always has an incentive to deviate — contradicting the Nash Equilibrium condition (no firm has an incentive to deviate). Therefore no asymmetric outcome with $0 < k < N$ can be a NE.
$$\text{Nash Equilibria} \in \{\text{all } T,\; \text{all } M\} \quad \text{only.}$$
∎ Q.E.D.
Key Results

Three Wage Scenarios

  • W1 (low wages — HICs/high demand): Modernizing is profitable regardless of others' choices → all modernize is the NE. Destined to modernize.
  • W3 (very high wages — LICs stuck): Modernizing is unprofitable even if all others modernize → no modernization NE only. Never modernize.
  • W2 (intermediate — coordination needed): Profitable only IF all others also modernize. Coordination required. This is the "big push" scenario.
Policy

Solving the W2 Coordination Problem

  • Firms cannot solve the problem alone (externality).
  • Options: technological change (e.g., Kerala mobile phones), government subsidies targeting industries with strong externalities, or external/international support.
  • Other considerations: lock-in from increasing returns, linkages between industries, inequality (poverty blocks education investment).

Topic 11 · O-Ring Theory
Kremer · Assortative Matching · Complementarities
Background

O-Ring Theory — Origins

  • Developed by Michael Kremer.
  • Named after the 1986 Challenger disaster — a single faulty o-ring caused catastrophic failure.
  • Best intuitive model: an orchestra — the quality of the weakest player affects the whole.
  • Fits well with modern manufacturing and high-skill services.
Core Formula

O-Ring Production Function

$$F(q_1, \ldots, q_N) = B \prod_{i=1}^{N} q_i \qquad 0 \le q_i \le 1$$
Example with 2 inputs: $F(q_1, q_2) = B q_1 q_2$

Output is the product of all input qualities. This creates extreme complementarities: even one low-quality input dramatically reduces total output — "one bad apple spoils the bunch."

Key Result

Assortative Matching

  • High-quality inputs have higher marginal products in high-quality firms than in low-quality firms.
  • This drives assortative matching: like inputs group together.
  • Proof with two firms: assortative output = q_l² + q_h² > heterogeneous output = 2q_l·q_h (since (q_h−q_l)² > 0).
  • Conditions needed: inputs must be sufficiently imperfect substitutes AND production must be sufficiently complementary.
Proof 1

O-Ring Production Creates Complementarities: $\partial^2 F / \partial q_i \partial q_j > 0$

Setup
We want to show that the marginal product of worker $i$'s quality is increasing in the quality of every other worker $j \neq i$. This is the formal definition of complementarity.
Step 1
The O-Ring production function with $N$ workers and $B = 1$:
$$F(q_1, \ldots, q_N) = \prod_{i=1}^{N} q_i$$
Step 2
Take the partial derivative with respect to $q_i$. Since $q_i$ appears exactly once as a multiplicative factor:
$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial q_i} = \prod_{j \neq i} q_j$$
This is the marginal product of worker $i$'s quality — it equals the product of all other workers' qualities.
Step 3
Now differentiate again with respect to any other worker $q_k$ (where $k \neq i$):
$$\frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial q_i \, \partial q_k} = \frac{\partial}{\partial q_k}\!\left(\prod_{j \neq i} q_j\right) = \prod_{j \neq i,\, j \neq k} q_j$$
Step 4
Since all qualities $q_j \in (0, 1]$, the product of any subset of them is strictly positive:
$$\frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial q_i \, \partial q_k} = \prod_{j \neq i,\, j \neq k} q_j > 0$$
The marginal product of worker $i$ strictly increases with every other worker's quality. This confirms strong complementarities across all pairs of inputs.
∎ Q.E.D.
Proof 2

Assortative Matching Theorem: Total Output Is Higher Under Like-with-Like Grouping

Setup
Two firms, each requiring exactly 2 workers. Four workers available: two high-skill ($q_H$) and two low-skill ($q_L$), with $q_H > q_L > 0$. $B = 1$, so $F(a, b) = ab$.
Step 1
Compute total output under assortative matching (Firm 1: $q_H, q_H$; Firm 2: $q_L, q_L$):
$$\Pi_A = q_H \cdot q_H + q_L \cdot q_L = q_H^2 + q_L^2$$
Step 2
Compute total output under heterogeneous matching (Firm 1: $q_H, q_L$; Firm 2: $q_H, q_L$):
$$\Pi_H = q_H \cdot q_L + q_H \cdot q_L = 2q_H q_L$$
Step 3
Compute the difference $\Pi_A - \Pi_H$:
$$\Pi_A - \Pi_H = q_H^2 + q_L^2 - 2q_H q_L = (q_H - q_L)^2$$
Step 4
Since $q_H > q_L$, we have $(q_H - q_L)^2 > 0$, therefore:
$$\Pi_A > \Pi_H \quad \text{for all } q_H \neq q_L$$
Assortative matching strictly dominates heterogeneous matching. The efficiency gap grows with $|q_H - q_L|$ — the wider the skill gap, the bigger the payoff from sorting.
Why firms sort
The high-skill firm's marginal value of hiring a high-skill worker is $\partial F / \partial q_2 \big|_{q_1=q_H} = q_H$. The low-skill firm's marginal value is $\partial F / \partial q_2 \big|_{q_1=q_L} = q_L$. Since $q_H > q_L$, the high-skill firm always outbids the low-skill firm for the high-quality worker — assortative matching is the only competitive equilibrium.
∎ Q.E.D.
Implications

O-Ring Theory Implications

  • Firms hire workers of similar skill levels (explains law firms, consulting firms, etc.).
  • Same task is paid more in higher-skill firms → explains extreme income inequality.
  • Surrounding skill levels raise incentives to learn.
  • Creates low-production poverty traps: poor institutions can't attract high-quality inputs (e.g., poor hospital can't hire top neurosurgeon).
  • Contrast: additive production function (F = q₁ + q₂) has no complementarities — marginal product is always 1, assortative matching irrelevant.

Topic 12 · Population
Demographics · Transition · Malthus
Facts

World Population Overview

  • ~7.87 billion in 2021; projected 9.6B by 2050, 11B by 2100.
  • Growth concentrated in developing world (gains ~97% of new individuals).
  • Population growth driven primarily by falling death rates, not rising birth rates.
Key Metrics

Components of Population Growth

  • Crude Birth Rate: Births per 1,000 people per year — dramatically higher in LICs.
  • Total Fertility Rate (TFR): Children born to a woman in her lifetime. Has fallen sharply in much of the developing world.
  • Death Rate: Deaths per 1,000 per year. At historic lows due to control of famine, disease, plague.
  • Dependency Ratio: Ratio of youth (0-14) + elderly (65+) to working-age (15-64) population. LICs have much higher youth dependency; aging HICs have higher elderly dependency.
Concept

Population Pyramids & Hidden Momentum

  • Wide-base pyramids (LICs) slow the rate at which lower fertility rates reduce population growth.
  • More people are in childbearing age cohorts → even as fertility falls, total births can keep rising.
  • This is called the Hidden Momentum of Population Growth.
Core Framework

Demographic Transition (3 Stages)

  • Stage 1: Low birth and death rates → low population growth.
  • Stage 2: Death rates fall first, birth rates lag → rapid population growth.
  • Stage 3: Birth rates also fall → population stabilizes.
  • Developing countries differ from classic transition — faster death rate declines without the economic development that drove earlier demographic transitions.
  • Replacement fertility: TFR that results in stable population levels (depends on death rate).
Debate

Is Population Growth a Problem?

  • Ehrlich (Population Bomb, 1968): Population imperils well-being; predicted 1970s famines (wrong).
  • Simon (The Ultimate Resource, 1981): Human innovation provides sufficient resources — closer to modern consensus.
  • Consensus view (Todaro & Smith): Population growth is NOT the primary cause of poverty; rapid growth or extreme density can inhibit development. Focus on poverty, not population per se.
  • Solow model link: n (pop growth) acts like depreciation — high n requires higher savings to maintain per capita capital.
Historical

Malthusian Poverty Trap

  • Thomas Malthus (1798): population grows geometrically, food supply arithmetically → eventual famine/catastrophe.
  • Not a formal model — no individual actors or incentives, just hypothesized relationships.
  • Empirically fails: technological progress has consistently generated sufficient additional resources.
  • Reason economics is called the "Dismal Science"!
Proof 1

Why Population Growth $n$ Acts Like Depreciation in Solow

Claim
In the per capita capital accumulation equation, $n$ appears additively with $\delta$: both erode per capita capital at the same rate. We prove this from first principles.
Step 1
Start from the aggregate capital accumulation equation:
$$\dot{K} = sY - \delta K$$
where $\dot{K} = dK/dt$ in continuous time.
Step 2
Define per capita capital $k \equiv K/L$. Differentiate using the quotient rule:
$$\dot{k} = \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{K}{L}\right) = \frac{\dot{K}L - K\dot{L}}{L^2} = \frac{\dot{K}}{L} - \frac{K}{L}\cdot\frac{\dot{L}}{L}$$
Step 3
Since $\dot{L}/L = n$ (population grows at rate $n$) and $K/L = k$:
$$\dot{k} = \frac{\dot{K}}{L} - nk$$
Step 4
Substitute $\dot{K} = sY - \delta K$ and divide by $L$:
$$\dot{k} = \frac{sY - \delta K}{L} - nk = sf(k) - \delta k - nk$$
Result
Collecting terms:
$$\boxed{\dot{k} = sf(k) - (\delta + n)k}$$
Both $\delta$ and $n$ appear symmetrically. $\delta k$ represents physical wear on existing capital; $nk$ represents the capital that must be created just to equip new workers at the existing level. Both "eat into" per capita capital growth at the same rate per unit of $k$.
∎ Q.E.D.
Proof 2

Formal Rebuttal of the Malthusian Trap: Technology Shifts the Constraint

Malthus's Claim
Let $P(t) = P_0 \cdot r^t$ (geometric, $r > 1$) be population and $F(t) = F_0 + at$ (arithmetic) be food supply. Then:
$$\frac{F(t)}{P(t)} = \frac{F_0 + at}{P_0 \cdot r^t} \xrightarrow{t \to \infty} 0$$
Per capita food supply eventually collapses to zero — catastrophe is inevitable.
Rebuttal 1
Technology makes food supply endogenous. Suppose agricultural productivity grows at rate $g_A > 0$: $F(t) = F_0 \cdot e^{g_A t}$. Then:
$$\frac{F(t)}{P(t)} = \frac{F_0 \cdot e^{g_A t}}{P_0 \cdot r^t} = \frac{F_0}{P_0} \cdot e^{(g_A - \ln r)t}$$
If $g_A > \ln r = n$ (the population growth rate), per capita food supply grows without bound — the Malthusian catastrophe never arrives.
Rebuttal 2
Demographic transition makes $n$ endogenous. As incomes rise, $n$ falls. Let $n = n(y)$ where $n'(y) < 0$. In the Solow model:
$$k^* = \left(\frac{sA}{\delta + n(y^*)}\right)^{\!\frac{1}{1-\alpha}}$$
Higher income → lower $n$ → lower $(\delta + n)$ → higher $k^*$ → still higher income. The feedback is stabilising, not explosive as Malthus assumed.
Rebuttal 3
No micro-foundations. Malthus offers no model of individual fertility choice. A proper economic model (e.g., Becker's quantity-quality tradeoff) predicts that as child-rearing costs rise with income, parents choose fewer, higher-quality children — exactly the observed demographic transition pattern.
∎ Q.E.D.

Topic 13 · Inequality
Kuznets Curve · Lorenz Curve · Gini Coefficient
Overview

Why Does Extreme Inequality Matter?

  • Economic inefficiencies: The poor cannot access credit → underinvestment in education, health, business. Lower national savings (highest savers are the middle class). Capital flight — wealthy spend on imported luxury goods.
  • Social instability: Encourages rent-seeking and exploitation by political elites. Concentrated political power → populist revolutions, civil strife.
  • Fairness (Rawls's Veil of Ignorance): If you had to choose the income distribution of your country before knowing which family you'd be born into, most people prefer more equal distributions than those observed in LICs.
Model

Kuznets Curve — Development & Inequality

  • Simon Kuznets (1950s): As development proceeds, inequality first worsens then improves — the famous inverted-U shape.
  • Intuition: Early industrialization pulls workers from low-inequality agriculture into high-variance urban sector, widening gaps. Later, as most workers are urban and institutions mature, inequality narrows.
  • Modern critique: Kuznets's results were largely driven by Latin American data. Many countries (Taiwan, South Korea, Costa Rica) developed without worsening inequality first. The inverted-U is no longer considered a universal law.
Concept

Types of Inequality Measures

  • Size Distributions: Rank individuals by income; assess shares accruing to each quintile/decile. Most commonly used.
  • Functional Distributions: Income accruing to factors of production (labor, land, capital). E.g., fraction of GDP paid in wages.
  • Weighted Measures: Intentionally over-weight certain parts of the distribution (e.g., the poor).
  • Group Gap Measures: Compare average income across demographic groups — gender gap, racial wage gap, etc. Most common in the press; do not satisfy anonymity.
Core Formula

Kuznets Ratio

$$\text{Kuznets Ratio} = \frac{\text{Income share of top 20\%}}{\text{Income share of bottom 40\%}}$$
  • Perfect equality: Top 20% have 20% of income, bottom 40% have 40% → ratio = 0.5.
  • Perfect inequality: Top 20% have 100%, bottom 40% have 0% → ratio → ∞.
  • Example: Top 20% = 50%, bottom 40% = 14% → Ratio = 50/14 = 3.57.
  • Higher ratio = more inequality.
Core Formula

Gini Coefficient & Lorenz Curve

  • Lorenz Curve: Plot cumulative population share (x-axis, ordered by income) against cumulative income share (y-axis). The line of equality is the 45° diagonal.
  • Gini Coefficient: Ratio of the area between the line of equality and the Lorenz curve to the area of the entire triangle below the line of equality.
$$G = \frac{A}{A+B}$$
$A$ = area between line of equality and Lorenz curve
$A+B$ = total triangle area = 0.5
So: $G = 2A = 1 - 2B$
  • $G = 0$: perfect equality (Lorenz = 45° line).
  • $G = 1$: perfect inequality (one person has everything).
  • More bowed out the Lorenz curve → higher Gini.

Proof

Why $G = 1 - 2\int_0^1 L(x)\,dx$ (Continuous Gini Formula)

Setup
Let $L(x)$ be the Lorenz curve, where $x \in [0,1]$ is the cumulative population share and $L(x)$ is the cumulative income share. The line of equality is $y = x$.
Step 1
Area $B$ (under the Lorenz curve):
$$B = \int_0^1 L(x)\,dx$$
Step 2
Total triangle area (under line of equality $y=x$):
$$A + B = \int_0^1 x\,dx = \frac{1}{2}$$
Step 3
Area $A$ = triangle area $-$ area $B$:
$$A = \frac{1}{2} - B = \frac{1}{2} - \int_0^1 L(x)\,dx$$
Step 4
$G = A/(A+B) = A/\tfrac{1}{2} = 2A$:
$$\boxed{G = 2A = 1 - 2\int_0^1 L(x)\,dx}$$
If $L(x) = x$ (perfect equality): $G = 1 - 2\int_0^1 x\,dx = 1 - 1 = 0$. ✓
∎ Q.E.D.

Topic 14 · Poverty
Poverty Lines · Headcount · Poverty Gap · FGT Index
Concept

Poverty Lines

  • A poverty line $Y_p$ is an income threshold — families below are poor, above are not.
  • Can be set globally (World Bank $1/day, $2/day), nationally, or locally. Usually adjusted for PPP.
  • Nutrition-based method (most common): Based on Engel's Law — the share of income spent on food falls as income rises. Steps: (1) estimate income-food-share relationship, (2) calculate cost of minimum nutrition, (3) find income level at which families can afford it.
  • UN minimum: cost of 2,000 calories/day. US USDA: Thrifty Food Plan meeting macro/micro-nutrient requirements.
Notation

Basic Notation

$N$ = total population  |  $i \in \{1,\ldots,N\}$ = individuals
$Y_i$ = income of person $i$  |  $Y_p$ = poverty line
$H$ = number of poor = $\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{1}[Y_i < Y_p]$
Core Formula

Headcount Measures

$$H = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbf{1}_{[Y_i < Y_p]} \qquad \text{(Headcount Statistic — number of poor)}$$ $$HI = \frac{H}{N} \qquad \text{(Headcount Index — fraction of population poor)}$$
  • Limitation: HI says nothing about how poor the poor are. Lifting someone just below the line to just above it reduces HI by the same amount as lifting the destitute.
Core Formula

Poverty Gap Measures

$$TPG = \sum_{i=1}^{H}(Y_p - Y_i) \qquad \text{(Total Poverty Gap)}$$ $$APG = \frac{TPG}{N} \quad NPG = \frac{APG}{Y_p} \quad AIS = \frac{TPG}{H} \quad NIS = \frac{AIS}{Y_p}$$
APG = average shortfall across all individuals  |  NPG = APG as % of poverty line
AIS = average shortfall among the poor only  |  NIS = AIS as % of poverty line
Core Formula

Foster-Greer-Thorbecke (FGT) Index

$$P_\alpha = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{H}\left(\frac{Y_p - Y_i}{Y_p}\right)^\alpha$$
  • $P_0$ ($\alpha=0$): each term = 1 → $P_0 = H/N = HI$ (Headcount Index)
  • $P_1$ ($\alpha=1$): each term = $(Y_p-Y_i)/Y_p$ → $P_1 = NPG$ (Normalized Poverty Gap)
  • $P_2$ ($\alpha=2$): Squared Poverty Gap — weights the poorest most heavily:
$$P_2 = \frac{H}{N}\left[NIS^2 + (1-NIS)^2 CV_P^2\right]$$
$CV_P$ = coefficient of variation among the poor. Incorporates inequality within the poor.

Topic 15 · Properties of Inequality & Poverty Measures
Anonymity · Scale · Population · Transfer · Monotonicity · Distributional Sensitivity
Concept

The Inconsistency Problem

  • Different measures can rank countries differently — this does not mean one is right and another wrong.
  • Root cause: some income distribution comparisons are inherently ambiguous — e.g., crossing Lorenz curves (Country A has a more equal bottom half, Country B has a more equal top half — no unambiguous ranking).
  • Solution: understand the properties each measure satisfies, then choose the measure appropriate to your purpose.
Inequality

Four Properties of Inequality Measures

  • Anonymity: Measure doesn't depend on who holds the income — only the distribution matters. Most measures satisfy this; Group Gap measures do not.
  • Scale Independence: Doubling everyone's income doesn't change inequality. Measured the same regardless of total economy size or currency. Both Kuznets and Gini satisfy this.
  • Population Independence: Cloning the entire population doesn't change inequality. China shouldn't be penalised for being larger than Canada. Both satisfy this.
  • Transfer Principle: Transferring income from a richer to a poorer person (without reversing their ranks) must reduce measured inequality. Kuznets fails — within-group transfers are invisible. Gini satisfies this.

Poverty

Four Properties of Poverty Measures

  • Anonymity: Same as for inequality — only the income distribution matters, not identities. All measures satisfy this.
  • Population Independence: Cloning the population leaves the measure unchanged. $H$ and $TPG$ fail (they double); $HI$ and $APG$ satisfy this.
  • Monotonicity: Giving income to a poor person must reduce measured poverty. $H$ and $HI$ fail (only react when someone crosses the line). $TPG$ and $APG$ satisfy this.
  • Distributional Sensitivity: Transferring from a poor person to a less-poor person must increase measured poverty. None of H, HI, TPG, APG satisfies this. Only $P_2$ (FGT with $\alpha=2$) does, via the $CV_P^2$ term.

Policy

Concluding Topics

  • Choosing the right measure: To target aid by country size → use $H$ or $TPG$ (no population independence desired). To compare economic conditions across countries → use $HI$, $APG$, $NPG$, $AIS$, $NIS$ (population independence needed).
  • Multidimensional Poverty Index (UNDP): Three dimensions — health, education, standard of living — measured at household level and aggregated. Analogous to HDI but for individuals.
  • Policy options: In-kind transfers (food stamps, educational grants), targeted subsidies (food/fuel), incentivized transfers (conditional cash transfers — CCTs), direct financial transfers (minimum income guarantees).
Proof

Why Kuznets Ratio Fails the Transfer Principle (Within Groups)

Setup
The Kuznets Ratio uses only two group aggregates: the top 20% and the bottom 40%. Transfers within either group change no group aggregate.
Example
Consider three quintiles: Top 20% = 40, Second 20% = 10, Bottom 20% = 10. Kuznets Ratio = 40/20 = 2. Now transfer $1 from Second to Bottom (richer to poorer within the bot 40%):
New: Top 20% = 40, Second 20% = 9, Bottom 20% = 11
New Kuznets Ratio = 40/(9+11) = 40/20 = 2. Unchanged!
The transfer correctly made the economy more equal, but the Kuznets Ratio is blind to it — it violates the transfer principle.
Gini
The Gini integrates over the entire Lorenz curve, so any redistribution that moves the Lorenz curve toward the 45° line — no matter where in the distribution — necessarily reduces $G$. Thus Gini satisfies the transfer principle for all transfers that don't reverse income ranks.

Topic 16 · Urbanization I
Harris-Todaro Model · Urban Unemployment · Expected Wages
Background

Lewis Two-Sector Model — Limitations

  • Lewis assumes workers can instantly move between rural and urban sectors depending on job availability.
  • Problem 1: Urban migration massively outstrips labor demand — creates large unemployment, which Lewis ignores.
  • Problem 2: Rural wages appear to be set at marginal product, not a subsistence floor.
  • Harris-Todaro (1970) corrects both by modeling the decision to migrate under uncertainty.
Core Model

Harris-Todaro Structure

  • Two sectors: Agriculture (competitive, flexible wages) and Manufacturing (fixed wage above market).
  • Manufacturing wage $\bar{W}_M$ is fixed above equilibrium — e.g., by minimum wage law.
  • Factories hire from the pool of urban residents (not from the countryside directly).
  • Migrants move without a job guarantee — they gamble on getting hired.
  • Two types of urban workers: employed (hired) and unemployed (searching).
Core Formula

Equilibrium Condition — Expected Wage

A rural worker will migrate as long as the expected urban wage exceeds the agricultural wage $W_A$.

$$E[W_{\text{urban}}] = \frac{L_M}{L_{US}} \cdot \bar{W}_M$$
$L_M$ = number of manufacturing jobs (fixed)  |  $L_{US}$ = total urban labor supply (employed + unemployed)

Migration continues until equilibrium:

$$W_A = \frac{L_M}{L_{US}} \cdot \bar{W}_M$$
  • $\bar{W}_M$ and $L_M$ are fixed. Only $L_{US}$ adjusts — through the unemployment pool $L_{US} = L_M + L_{\text{unemployed}}$.
  • Higher unemployment = lower probability of getting hired = lower expected wage → equilibrates the market.

Counter-Intuitive Implications

  • Raising manufacturing wages increases unemployment: Higher $\bar{W}_M$ → higher expected urban wage → more rural migrants → bigger unemployment pool to restore equilibrium.
  • Creating more manufacturing jobs can increase unemployment: More jobs ($\uparrow L_M$) → higher probability of employment → more people migrate → net unemployment may rise if migration > job creation.

Proof

Deriving the Equilibrium Urban Labor Supply $L_{US}^*$

Given
Equilibrium condition: $W_A = \frac{L_M}{L_{US}} \bar{W}_M$. Solve for $L_{US}^*$.
Step 1
Rearrange:
$$L_{US}^* = \frac{L_M \cdot \bar{W}_M}{W_A}$$
Step 2
Urban unemployment in equilibrium:
$$L_{\text{unemp}}^* = L_{US}^* - L_M = L_M\left(\frac{\bar{W}_M}{W_A} - 1\right) = L_M \cdot \frac{\bar{W}_M - W_A}{W_A}$$
Step 3
Unemployment rate:
$$u^* = \frac{L_{\text{unemp}}^*}{L_{US}^*} = 1 - \frac{W_A}{\bar{W}_M}$$
As $\bar{W}_M \uparrow$, $u^* \uparrow$. As $W_A \uparrow$, $u^* \downarrow$. More jobs ($\uparrow L_M$) raises $L_{US}^*$ by the same factor, leaving $u^*$ unchanged — but total unemployment rises.
∎ Q.E.D.
Topic 17 · Urbanization II
Agglomeration · Giantism · Slums · Informal Sector
Concept

Why Do Cities Exist? Agglomeration Economies

  • Urban clustering: The tendency of related businesses, workers, and consumers to co-locate to reduce costs.
  • Localization economies: Cost advantages specific to firms in the same industry co-locating — lower input transport costs, specialized suppliers (e.g., auto-parts suppliers around Detroit).
  • Urbanization economies: General advantages of being in a city — social capital, shared infrastructure, thick labor markets, knowledge spillovers.
  • Location often determined by historical accident (Silicon Valley), trade routes (Chicago), or initial specialization.
Concept

Limits to Urban Scale — Congestion

  • Congestion = "an action by one agent that decreases incentives for other agents to take similar actions."
  • Land: Becomes scarce and expensive → builds vertically instead of horizontally.
  • Transportation: Commuting distances and times increase.
  • Wages: Must rise to compensate workers for higher cost of living → raises all prices.
  • Result: Cities are naturally bounded — HICs have multiple cities rather than one mega-city.
Key Concept

Urban Giantism Paradox

LICs often have disproportionately large primate cities — the "giantism paradox." Three causes:

  • First-city bias: Once a city establishes dominance, increasing returns reinforce its lead.
  • Protectionism: Without trade, production must locate near the largest domestic market (the biggest city), attracting more workers there in a self-reinforcing spiral.
  • Political economy: Dictators concentrate resources in capital cities to prevent urban unrest and maintain patronage. Countries with unstable authoritarian regimes have systematically larger capital cities.
Concept

Urban Slums

  • ~1/3 of all urban residents in developing countries live in slums (dense housing failing basic standards).
  • UN definition: A slum household lacks at least one of: (1) durable housing, (2) sufficient living space (≤3 per room), (3) safe water access, (4) adequate sanitation, (5) security of tenure.
  • Why do slums exist? Poverty + weak institutions. Poor cannot afford code-compliant housing; building codes exist in most countries but enforcement is weak due to corruption, limited government capacity, and the large fraction of individuals unable to afford minimum housing.
  • Lack of tenure security → no incentive to invest in improving housing.
  • 30-40% of non-permanent slum housing is in disaster-prone zones.
Exam Focus

Informal Sector

  • Sector that does not formally register, pay taxes, or receive government benefits. Represents 30-70% of urban employment in LICs.
  • Positives: Generates employment without state support; labor-intensive (good when capital is scarce); includes recycling, low-cost training, black markets that improve efficiency around price controls.
  • Problems: No regulation → environmental harm, safety issues, no benefits. Encourages slum development. Women are disproportionately concentrated here.
  • Policy: Reduce registration red tape; subsidize/develop key informal industries; facilitate credit access.
Worked Example

Giantism via Political Economy: First-City Feedback Loop

Country has 2 cities: Capital C₁ (population 2M) and secondary C₂ (population 0.5M). A dictator routes 80% of infrastructure investment to C₁.

Round 1: C₁ gets better roads, hospitals, jobs → firms prefer C₁ → 200k people migrate from C₂
Round 2: C₁ now has 2.2M → even larger consumer base → more firms → more infrastructure
$$\text{First-city share} = \frac{2.2}{2.2+0.3} = 88\% \quad (\text{up from } \frac{2}{2.5} = 80\%)$$

This is the paradox: the largest city grows fastest not because it is most efficient, but because political distortions create self-reinforcing agglomeration. The efficient city sizes predicted by agglomeration economies alone are never reached.

Topic 18 · Environment I
Tragedy of the Commons · Public Goods · Property Rights
Framework

Economists' Approach to Environmental Problems

  • Pollution and resource depletion are not inherently bad — they are costs that must be weighed against the utility of the activities that produce them.
  • Focus: find the market failure causing over-production of pollutants or over-use of resources, then correct it.
  • Goal is efficiency (Pareto optimality) — not necessarily zero pollution.
Concept

Perfect Property Rights — Four Criteria

The fundamental theorem of welfare economics (markets are Pareto optimal) requires perfect property rights:

Universality — all resources are privately owned
Excludability — owner can prevent others from using the resource
Transferability — ownership can be reassigned (bought/sold)
Enforceability — ownership rights and contracts can be enforced

Environmental problems arise when one or more of these fails — most importantly, excludability.

Core Model

Tragedy of the Commons

  • Applies to goods that are rivalrous (my use reduces value to others) but non-excludable (no one can be prevented from using).
  • Examples: fisheries, grazing land, aquifers, shared oil fields.
  • With excludability (owned fishery): Owner maximises profit → chooses $L^*$ fishermen (Pareto optimal).
  • Without excludability (open fishery): Any fisherman fishes as long as average product ≥ wage $w$. Results in $L_C > L^*$ (over-use), with marginal product below wage $w$ → workers could be more productively employed elsewhere.
$$\text{Owner's condition: } MP_L = w \quad \Rightarrow \quad L^* \text{ (efficient)}$$ $$\text{Open access condition: } AP_L = w \quad \Rightarrow \quad L_C > L^* \text{ (over-use)}$$
Core Model

Public Goods & Free Rider Problem

  • Public goods: Non-rivalrous (my use doesn't reduce yours) AND non-excludable.
  • Examples: clean air, national defense, lighthouses, a university tower (the UT Tower example).
  • Demand aggregation: For rivalrous goods, aggregate demand = horizontal sum of individual demands. For public goods, aggregate demand = vertical sum (everyone benefits from the same unit simultaneously).
  • Free rider problem: If others will provide the good anyway, each individual prefers to pay nothing and free-ride. Neither person provides the social optimum → under-provision.
  • The social optimum requires that the sum of marginal valuations equals marginal cost: $\sum_i MB_i = MC$.
$$\text{Rivalrous: } Q^* \text{ where } MB_A(Q^*) = P_M \text{ or } MB_B(Q^*) = P_M$$ $$\text{Non-rivalrous: } Q^* \text{ where } MB_A(Q^*) + MB_B(Q^*) = P_M$$

Let total output $Y = f(L)$ with $f' > 0$, $f'' < 0$ (increasing but concave). Marginal product: $MP_L = f'(L)$. Average product: $AP_L = f(L)/L$.
Step 1
Owner's optimal: $MP_L(L^*) = w$, so $f'(L^*) = w$.
Step 2
Open access: fishermen enter until average product equals wage: $AP_L(L_C) = w$, so $f(L_C)/L_C = w$.
Step 3
Show $AP_L > MP_L$ for all $L > 0$ when $f$ is concave. By the mean value theorem applied to $f$:
$$\frac{f(L)}{L} = \frac{f(L) - f(0)}{L - 0} = f'(\xi) \text{ for some } \xi \in (0,L)$$
Since $f'' < 0$, $f'$ is decreasing, so $f'(\xi) > f'(L)$ for $\xi < L$. Therefore:
$$AP_L(L) = f'(\xi) > f'(L) = MP_L(L) \quad \forall\, L > 0$$
Step 4
At $L^*$: $MP_L(L^*) = w$ and $AP_L(L^*) > MP_L(L^*) = w$. So open-access fishermen still find it profitable to enter at $L^*$ (average product exceeds wage). They keep entering until $AP_L(L_C) = w$, with $L_C > L^*$.
$$\boxed{L_C > L^*} \quad \text{Open access always over-uses the resource.}$$
∎ Q.E.D.
Worked Example

Finding $L^*$ and $L_C$ with $f(L) = A\sqrt{L}$

Let $f(L) = 10\sqrt{L}$, wage $w = 2$.

$MP_L = f'(L) = \frac{10}{2\sqrt{L}} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{L}}$

Owner's optimum: $\frac{5}{\sqrt{L^*}} = 2 \Rightarrow \sqrt{L^*} = 2.5 \Rightarrow L^* = 6.25$

$AP_L = \frac{f(L)}{L} = \frac{10}{\sqrt{L}}$

Open access: $\frac{10}{\sqrt{L_C}} = 2 \Rightarrow \sqrt{L_C} = 5 \Rightarrow L_C = 25$

$L_C = 25$ vs $L^* = 6.25$ — open access attracts 4× too many fishermen! Output at $L^*$: $f(6.25) = 25$. Output at $L_C$: $f(25) = 50$. Total wages at $L_C$: $25 \times 2 = 50$. Profit = 0. The entire surplus is dissipated — the fishery generates no net value above the outside option.

Topic 19 · Environment II
Particulate Matter · Global Warming · Water · Slums · Policy
Core Concept

Particulate Matter (PM)

  • Definition: Very small particles of matter under 10 microns in size. The size of the particle matters most — composition is secondary.
  • PM10 vs PM2.5: PM10 was measured first; PM2.5 introduced later. PM2.5 is more dangerous because smaller particles penetrate deeper.
  • Health mechanism: Small size lets particles evade the body's natural defenses (cilia, cellular barriers). They cause lung inflammation and enter the bloodstream — leading to cardio-pulmonary disorders, worsening asthma, bronchitis, and premature death.
  • Sources: Any combustion process — directly or via secondary reactions with NOx. Most problematic: industrial combustion, coal electricity generation, burning crop residue (huge problem in India), and indoor biomass burning for cooking/heating.
  • Key research (Duflo, Greenstone, Hanna): Indoor biomass burning is particularly dangerous for women and children in LICs — stoves without ventilation expose occupants to extreme PM concentrations daily.
Core Concept

Global Warming

Higher concentrations of CO2 and industrial chemicals (HFCs, CFCs) retain solar heat that would otherwise radiate back into space, raising Earth's temperature.

  • Primary challenges: Rising sea levels, greater temperature variation, more frequent and powerful storms, shifts in local climate patterns, reduced agricultural productivity in semi-arid regions.
  • Rising sea levels: Devastating for low-lying islands (Oceania, Caribbean), forces migration, destroys property and infrastructure.
  • Storm intensity: Tropical cyclones more frequent and powerful — especially dangerous in LICs with poor construction quality, fragile infrastructure, and limited disaster response capacity.
  • LICs uniquely vulnerable: High household reliance on farming and fishing. Poor construction cannot withstand disasters. Fragile political systems can fracture — the Darfur crisis may have been triggered by climate-driven drought.
  • Agricultural impact: Productivity of semi-arid regions falls (shorter growing seasons, adverse conditions). A few highland areas (e.g., Ethiopia highlands) may benefit — but net losses far exceed gains globally.
Concept

Water

  • Access to clean water increasingly called the new "oil" — central to international conflicts (China-India, Middle East).
  • Three major water issues in LICs:
    1. Pollution of waterways and shorelines — industrial waste, raw sewage, agricultural run-off.
    2. Open sewage systems in urban areas — flood risk, disease outbreak, contamination of groundwater.
    3. Lack of access to clean drinking water — tap water in many LIC cities is untreated and unsafe by HIC standards.
  • Glacial dependence: Many LIC populations depend on glacial meltwater for drinking and irrigation. Accelerating glacier retreat threatens long-run water security.
Concept

Slum Environments

The urban poor face a compounding set of environmental harms:

  • Air pollution: Indoor pollution from adjacent households. Outdoor pollution from nearby factories and automobiles — slums are often located near industrial zones.
  • Water: Open sewers that can flood. Lack of access to clean drinking water. Proximity to polluted waterways with untreated sewage and industrial waste.
  • Interactions: Environmental harms compound each other and interact with poverty — sick workers earn less, families cannot afford to move, and poor areas receive fewer municipal services.
Policy

Policy Options

  • A wide range of policy options exist — from carbon taxes and cap-and-trade to international agreements, technology subsidies, and infrastructure investments.
  • Key constraint: Solutions almost always require coordinated action by both developed and developing countries. Developed countries are historically responsible for most cumulative emissions; developing countries account for most future emissions growth.
  • This creates a fundamental collective action problem — individual country incentives diverge from global optimum (classic public goods/externality failure at international scale).

Topic 20 · Gender & Sex Ratios
Gender Inequality · Missing Women · Fischer's Principle · Son Preference
Overview

Gender Inequality in Development

  • Gender discrimination is a global problem but far more severe in LICs. The status of women is crucial for development:
  • Direct impact on wellbeing of half the population. Women make up half the potential labor force — discrimination inefficiently allocates jobs to relatively less productive men.
  • Women's status is directly associated with child health, education outcomes, and demographic transition.
  • Evidence sources: Economist Intelligence Unit Women's Economic Opportunity Index, UN Gender Inequality Index, legal rights data, household resource allocation surveys, cultural attitude surveys.
  • Key empirical facts: In many LICs, women cannot hold the same jobs as men by law; married men and women lack equal property rights; large fractions of women are excluded from decisions about their own income; significant shares of women agree wife-beating can be justified; majorities believe men make better leaders.
Core Concept

Missing Women

Term coined by Nobel laureate Amartya Sen. The observed ratio of males to females in many LICs is much higher than the biological norm — implying that a significant number of women are "missing" due to excess female mortality.

  • Causes by life stage:
    • At birth: Sex-selective abortion (ultrasound used to identify female fetuses) and infanticide — especially severe under China's one-child policy.
    • Ages 2–5: Oster (2008) finds this is where most of the imbalance in India emerges. Vaccination differences explain 20–30%; malnutrition explains 20%.
    • Adulthood: Unequal access to nutrition and medical care; high maternal mortality rates.
Core Formula

Missing Women Calculation

Let $r_a$ = actual sex ratio (males/females), $r_e$ = expected (biological) sex ratio, $m_a$ = actual males, $f_a$ = actual females, $f_e$ = expected females.

$$\frac{f_a}{f_e} = \frac{r_e}{r_a}$$ Natural birth ratio: $r_e = 1.06$

If actual birth ratio $r_a = 1.14$:
$$\frac{f_a}{f_e} = \frac{1.06}{1.14} \approx 0.930 \implies 7\% \text{ of girls are missing}$$
  • Assumption: The number of actual men equals expected men ($m_a = m_e$), so any deviation in the sex ratio must come from missing women.
  • China 2020 example: 10 million births, sex ratio at birth = 1.11. $f_a/f_e = 1.06/1.11 = 0.955$ → 4.5% of girls missing. With 4.7M girls born, expected = 4.9M → ~200,000 missing girls at birth in a single year.
Proof

Fischer's Principle: Why 1:1 Sex Ratio is a Stable Equilibrium

Setup
Some individuals have a genetic predisposition to produce more boys or more girls (like eye color). We ask: what sex ratio is evolutionarily stable?
Step 1
At ratio 1:1, men and women have equal probability of reproducing. The propensity to produce boys vs girls is equally represented — no sex has an advantage. This is an equilibrium.
Step 2
Suppose more women than men exist (ratio $< 1$). Men are scarce, so men find mates more easily — each man produces more offspring than each woman. Parents who produce more sons will have more grandchildren. Genes for producing boys spread through the population → ratio rises back toward 1.
Step 3
By the symmetric argument, if men are more common (ratio $> 1$), women find mates more easily. Genes for producing girls spread → ratio falls back toward 1. Therefore 1:1 is the unique evolutionarily stable sex ratio.
Implication
Any sustained deviation from 1:1 at sexual maturity is evidence of excess mortality in the underrepresented sex — it cannot be a biological equilibrium. In countries where adult sex ratios substantially exceed 1.0 (e.g., China ~1.06), the deviation implies missing women due to social, cultural, or policy factors.
∎ Q.E.D.
Exam Focus

Reasons for Son Preference & Consequences

Son preference drivers (patrilineal cultures):

  • Family names and inheritance rights pass through sons.
  • Old age support — sons expected to care for parents; daughters "lost" to husband's family.
  • Dowries — families must pay when daughters marry, making daughters a financial liability.
  • Better labor force opportunities for men → higher expected lifetime earnings.
  • Family, social, and religious pressure.

Qian (2008) — Economic status reduces son preference: In China, Maoist reforms promoted tea cultivation (favors smaller hands = women) vs orchard fruits (favors men). Tea areas → 10% more income for women → 1% more girls surviving to adulthood. Economic empowerment, not just policy, shapes sex ratios.

Consequences of skewed ratios:

  • Men: delayed or forgone marriage as women are simply scarce. Worsens with successive generations of skewed ratios. Poorest men most affected (women marry into higher-status families).
  • Women: families may over-emphasize women's roles as mothers and wives rather than economic actors — potentially worsening gender inequality.

Topic 21 · Agriculture I
Why Agriculture Matters · Structural Transformation · Three Steps
Overview

Why Agriculture Matters

  • Scale: 2 billion people involved in farming worldwide. 3.1 billion lived in rural areas in 2010 — with 1/4 in extreme poverty.
  • 2/3 of the world's poorest are located in rural areas, usually engaged in subsistence agriculture.
  • In 2009, 1 billion people did not have enough food — meaning they could not meet basic nutritional requirements.
  • Historical neglect: Classic models (Lewis) treated agriculture as passive — a source of surplus labour for manufacturing. Modern understanding: agriculture must develop alongside urban sectors, not just feed them.
  • Rural development elements: Improved productivity (technology, reorganisation, policy), increased demand through urban growth, diversification into non-farming rural activities.
Model

Structural Transformation (Timmer 2007)

As countries develop, agriculture's share of GDP and employment systematically falls. Four stages of transformation:

  1. Agricultural productivity surplus: Technology and organisation raise farm output above subsistence needs, creating a surplus.
  2. Surplus transferred to urban sector: Via investment flows, taxation of agriculture, or price controls on food — funds manufacturing growth.
  3. Progressive integration: Agricultural and non-agricultural input, output, and labour markets become progressively integrated — prices converge, workers move freely.
  4. Full development: Agriculture becomes just another sector in the economy — small share of GDP, highly productive, fully market-integrated.

Today in the US, there are more lawyers and dry cleaners than farmers — illustrating full structural transformation. Most LICs are in stages 1-2.

Topic 22 · Agriculture II
Productivity · Technology Adoption · Crop Insurance · Green Revolution
Core Concept

Agricultural Productivity Gaps

  • Agricultural productivity (output per worker and output per hectare) varies enormously across countries and regions.
  • Despite technological advances, productivity in developing countries — especially Africa — remains very low.
  • Two primary barriers to technology adoption:
    • Prevailing farm structures: In Latin America, farms are often too large to manage efficiently with available labour. In Africa, farms are too small to justify mechanisation — fixed costs can't be spread over enough output.
    • Agricultural poverty trap: Higher-yield crop varieties are more sensitive to environmental conditions and require more inputs — they are riskier. Farmers living near the subsistence level cannot tolerate the risk of crop failure. This traps them in low-productivity farming even when better technology exists.
Concept

Crop Insurance & Moral Hazard

  • Why crop insurance is difficult: Standard yield-based insurance (pays out based on volume grown on a plot) suffers from severe moral hazard — farmers can cut back on irrigation, fertiliser, and other investments knowing they'll be compensated for poor yields.
  • Rainfall insurance: Pays off based on the characteristics of rainfall (e.g., total rainfall in a season, number of dry days) rather than actual yield.
    • Advantage: Eliminates moral hazard — farmers cannot affect rainfall so they maintain full investment incentives.
    • Challenge 1 — Reinsurance: Rainfall is a regional risk. An insurer needs to distribute risk across many uncorrelated regions — requires significant scale.
    • Challenge 2 — Basis risk: Need to estimate the probability distribution of rainfall at the micro-climate level affecting specific farmers. Often numerous micro-climates with little historical data.
Core Concept

The Green Revolution

  • Historical context: English wheat yields rose from 0.5 to 2 tons/hectare between 900 AD and 1900 AD, then jumped to 7 tons/hectare in the 20th century — driven by fertilisers, plant breeding, pesticides, growing techniques, and mechanisation. Production in LICs did not increase as much during most of this period.
  • 1960s breakthrough: Agronomists targeted Asia with two key innovations:
    • HYV Wheat — Norman Borlaug (Nobel Peace Prize): stronger stalks to support heavier seed load, faster maturation (more growing seasons), wider temperature range, natural pest resistance.
    • HYV Rice — similar properties, targeting Asia's primary staple crop.
  • Result: Dramatic productivity increases across Asia. Expanded to other crops (maize, sorghum, cassava). Averted large-scale famine that Malthusian models predicted.
  • Limitations: Benefits concentrated in Asia (particularly Punjab region of India/Pakistan) — Africa benefited less. HYV varieties require fertiliser and irrigation, which poor farmers lack access to, potentially exacerbating inequality within agriculture.
Worked Example

Agricultural Poverty Trap — Risk and Expected Utility

A subsistence farmer considers switching to HYV seeds. Current crop (traditional): yield = 10 with certainty. HYV: yield = 20 with probability 0.6, yield = 3 with probability 0.4. Subsistence minimum = 5.

Expected yield (HYV) $= 0.6 \times 20 + 0.4 \times 3 = 12 + 1.2 = 13.2 > 10$ (traditional)

But: probability of falling below subsistence with HYV $= 0.4$
With traditional seeds: probability of below subsistence $= 0$

If a farmer uses log utility: $U(y) = \ln(y)$:
$EU(\text{HYV}) = 0.6\ln(20) + 0.4\ln(3) = 0.6(3.00) + 0.4(1.10) = 1.80 + 0.44 = 2.24$
$U(\text{traditional}) = \ln(10) = 2.30$

Risk-averse farmer prefers traditional seeds despite lower expected yield!

This illustrates why the agricultural poverty trap is rational, not a failure of information. The solution is crop insurance — if the 0.4 probability downside is insured, expected utility of HYV rises above traditional. This is why insurance markets are so critical for agricultural development.
